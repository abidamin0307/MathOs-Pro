How to Solve Quadratic Equations: A Complete Step-by-Step Guide

Quadratic equations appear everywhere in science, engineering, and finance. From calculating the trajectory of a rocket to determining optimal pricing for a product, the quadratic formula is one of the most useful tools in mathematics. In this guide, you'll learn not just how to use the quadratic formula, but why it works and how to apply it in real situations.

What is a Quadratic Equation?

A quadratic equation is any equation that can be written in the form ax² + bx + c = 0, where a, b, and c are constants and a ≠ 0. The name "quadratic" comes from "quadratus," the Latin word for square, because the variable gets squared (x²). The highest power of x is 2, which makes it a second-degree polynomial.

The Quadratic Formula

The quadratic formula solves for x: x = [-b ± √(b² - 4ac)] / 2a. The symbol ± means you get two solutions: one with plus, one with minus. The expression inside the square root, b² - 4ac, is called the discriminant. It tells you the nature of the solutions:

  • If discriminant > 0: Two distinct real roots.
  • If discriminant = 0: One real root (a repeated root).
  • If discriminant < 0: Two complex roots (involving imaginary numbers).

Step-by-Step Example

Solve 2x² - 4x - 6 = 0. Here a=2, b=-4, c=-6. Discriminant = (-4)² - 4×2×(-6) = 16 + 48 = 64. √64 = 8. Then x = [4 ± 8] / (4). So x = (12)/4 = 3, or x = (-4)/4 = -1. The equation has roots 3 and -1.

Real-World Applications

Quadratic equations model projectile motion (height of a thrown ball over time), area optimization (maximizing rectangle area with fixed perimeter), and profit maximization in business. They also appear in optics, electronics, and civil engineering.

Understanding BMI: What Your Number Really Means for Your Health

Body Mass Index (BMI) is a simple screening tool that categorizes weight relative to height. It's used by doctors, researchers, and fitness professionals worldwide. But what does your BMI actually tell you – and what are its limitations? This guide explains everything you need to know.

How BMI is Calculated

BMI = weight (kg) / height² (m²). For example, a person weighing 70 kg and 1.75 m tall has a BMI of 70 / (1.75×1.75) = 22.9, which falls in the "normal" range. The standard categories are: Underweight (<18.5), Normal (18.5–24.9), Overweight (25–29.9), Obese (≥30).

What BMI Does Well

At a population level, BMI correlates with body fat percentage and health risks like heart disease, diabetes, and hypertension. It's quick, free, and non-invasive, making it useful for large-scale studies and initial screenings.

Limitations of BMI

BMI doesn't distinguish between muscle and fat. Athletes with high muscle mass may have a high BMI but low body fat. It also doesn't account for fat distribution (visceral vs. subcutaneous), age, or ethnicity. That's why BMI is a screening tool, not a diagnostic one.

Mortgage Payment Explained: How to Calculate Your Monthly Payment

Buying a home is one of the biggest financial decisions you'll ever make. Understanding how mortgage payments work – and how much you'll actually pay each month – is crucial. This guide breaks down the math behind mortgages and shows you how to use our calculator to plan your budget.

The Mortgage Payment Formula

Monthly payment (M) = P × [ r(1+r)^n ] / [ (1+r)^n - 1 ], where P is the loan principal, r is the monthly interest rate (annual rate divided by 12), and n is the number of monthly payments (loan term in years × 12). This is called an amortizing loan – each payment covers both interest and principal.

Example Calculation

Suppose you borrow $300,000 at 4% annual interest for 30 years. Monthly rate r = 0.04/12 = 0.003333, n = 360. Plugging into the formula gives a monthly payment of approximately $1,432. Over 30 years, you'll pay $515,608 in total – meaning $215,608 in interest alone.

Factors That Affect Your Payment

  • Interest rate: A 1% difference can change your payment by hundreds of dollars.
  • Loan term: 15-year loans have higher monthly payments but much less total interest.
  • Down payment: A larger down payment reduces principal and may eliminate private mortgage insurance (PMI).

Reynolds Number: The Key to Understanding Fluid Flow

In engineering and physics, the Reynolds number predicts whether fluid flow will be smooth (laminar) or chaotic (turbulent). It's essential for designing pipes, aircraft wings, and even blood vessels. This guide explains the formula and its practical applications.

The Reynolds Number Formula

Re = ρvL/μ, where ρ is fluid density, v is velocity, L is characteristic length (e.g., pipe diameter), and μ is dynamic viscosity. The result is dimensionless – it has no units.

Interpreting the Value

  • Re < 2000: Laminar flow – smooth, predictable, low mixing.
  • 2000 < Re < 4000: Transitional flow – sometimes laminar, sometimes turbulent.
  • Re > 4000: Turbulent flow – chaotic, high mixing, more drag.

Why It Matters

Engineers use Reynolds number to scale experiments (dynamic similarity), design heat exchangers, calculate pressure drops in pipes, and predict drag on vehicles. For example, airplane wings are designed to delay turbulent flow to reduce drag.

Unit Conversion Guide: How to Convert Between Metric and Imperial Systems

Whether you're cooking, traveling, or working in engineering, converting units correctly is essential. This guide covers the most common conversions and explains why precision matters.

Length Conversions

1 meter = 3.28084 feet. To convert meters to feet, multiply by 3.28084. To convert feet to meters, divide by 3.28084. For small lengths, 1 inch = 2.54 cm exactly. Memorize these key numbers.

Weight Conversions

1 kilogram = 2.20462 pounds. A 70 kg person weighs about 154 lbs. For cooking, 1 ounce = 28.3495 grams. Precision matters in baking – use our converter for accuracy.

Temperature Conversions

Celsius to Fahrenheit: °F = (°C × 9/5) + 32. Fahrenheit to Celsius: °C = (°F - 32) × 5/9. Water freezes at 0°C (32°F) and boils at 100°C (212°F).

Kinetic Energy: The Physics of Motion Explained

Kinetic energy is the energy an object possesses due to its motion. The faster something moves, the more kinetic energy it has – and the more destructive it can be in a collision. This guide explains the formula and its real-world implications.

The Formula

KE = ½ × m × v², where m is mass in kilograms and v is velocity in meters per second. The result is in Joules. Notice that velocity is squared – doubling speed quadruples kinetic energy. That's why highway crashes are exponentially more dangerous than low-speed impacts.

Real-World Example

A car of mass 1500 kg traveling at 20 m/s (72 km/h) has KE = 0.5 × 1500 × 400 = 300,000 J. That's enough energy to lift a 300 kg weight 100 meters high. At 40 m/s (144 km/h), KE = 1,200,000 J – four times as much.

Applications

Engineers use kinetic energy calculations to design crumple zones, airbags, and crash barriers. In renewable energy, wind turbines capture kinetic energy from moving air. Even sports like baseball use kinetic energy – a fastball carries significant energy when it hits the bat.
