How to Solve Quadratic Equations: A Complete Step-by-Step Guide

Quadratic equations appear everywhere in science, engineering, and finance. From calculating the trajectory of a rocket to determining optimal pricing for a product, the quadratic formula is one of the most useful tools in mathematics. In this guide, you'll learn not just how to use the quadratic formula, but why it works and how to apply it in real situations.

What is a Quadratic Equation?

A quadratic equation is any equation that can be written in the form ax² + bx + c = 0, where a, b, and c are constants and a ≠ 0. The name "quadratic" comes from "quadratus," the Latin word for square, because the variable gets squared (x²). The highest power of x is 2, which makes it a second-degree polynomial.

The Quadratic Formula

The quadratic formula solves for x: x = [-b ± √(b² - 4ac)] / 2a. The symbol ± means you get two solutions: one with plus, one with minus. The expression inside the square root, b² - 4ac, is called the discriminant. It tells you the nature of the solutions:

  • If discriminant > 0: Two distinct real roots.
  • If discriminant = 0: One real root (a repeated root).
  • If discriminant < 0: Two complex roots (involving imaginary numbers).

Step-by-Step Example

Solve 2x² - 4x - 6 = 0. Here a=2, b=-4, c=-6. Discriminant = (-4)² - 4×2×(-6) = 16 + 48 = 64. √64 = 8. Then x = [4 ± 8] / (4). So x = (12)/4 = 3, or x = (-4)/4 = -1. The equation has roots 3 and -1.

Real-World Applications

Quadratic equations model projectile motion (height of a thrown ball over time), area optimization (maximizing rectangle area with fixed perimeter), and profit maximization in business. They also appear in optics, electronics, and civil engineering.

Understanding BMI: What Your Number Really Means for Your Health

Body Mass Index (BMI) is a simple screening tool that categorizes weight relative to height. It's used by doctors, researchers, and fitness professionals worldwide. But what does your BMI actually tell you – and what are its limitations? This guide explains everything you need to know.

How BMI is Calculated

BMI = weight (kg) / height² (m²). For example, a person weighing 70 kg and 1.75 m tall has a BMI of 70 / (1.75×1.75) = 22.9, which falls in the "normal" range. The standard categories are: Underweight (<18.5), Normal (18.5–24.9), Overweight (25–29.9), Obese (≥30).

What BMI Does Well

At a population level, BMI correlates with body fat percentage and health risks like heart disease, diabetes, and hypertension. It's quick, free, and non-invasive, making it useful for large-scale studies and initial screenings.

Limitations of BMI

BMI doesn't distinguish between muscle and fat. Athletes with high muscle mass may have a high BMI but low body fat. It also doesn't account for fat distribution (visceral vs. subcutaneous), age, or ethnicity. That's why BMI is a screening tool, not a diagnostic one.

How to Calculate Percentage Change: A Complete Guide

Percentage change is one of the most common calculations in daily life – from shopping discounts to salary raises, stock market moves, and population growth. Understanding percentage change helps you compare values over time and make informed decisions.

The Formula

Percentage Change = (New Value - Original Value) / Original Value × 100%

  • Positive result → Percentage increase
  • Negative result → Percentage decrease

Step-by-Step Example

A phone originally costs ₹20,000. After a sale, it costs ₹16,000. Change = (16,000 - 20,000) / 20,000 × 100 = (-4,000)/20,000 × 100 = -20%. That's a 20% discount.

Simple vs Compound Interest: What Every Investor Must Know

Interest is the cost of borrowing money or the reward for saving. But not all interest is equal. Simple interest is calculated only on the principal, while compound interest grows on both principal and previously earned interest – leading to exponential growth over time.

Simple Interest Formula

Simple Interest = Principal × Rate × Time
Example: ₹10,000 at 5% for 3 years = 10,000 × 0.05 × 3 = ₹1,500 total interest.

Compound Interest Formula

A = P(1 + r/n)nt where n = compounding frequency per year. The same ₹10,000 at 5% compounded annually for 3 years = 10,000(1.05)3 ≈ ₹11,576.25 – interest of ₹1,576.25.

Solving Linear Equations: A Step-by-Step Guide for Beginners

Linear equations are the building blocks of algebra. They appear everywhere – from calculating budgets to predicting trends. A linear equation looks like ax + b = 0, and solving it means finding the value of x that makes the equation true.

How to Solve

Standard Deviation: How to Measure Spread in Data

Standard deviation tells you how much data values deviate from the mean. A low standard deviation means data points are clustered close to the average; a high one means they're spread out. It's widely used in finance, research, and quality control.

Calculation Steps

  1. Find the mean (average).
  2. Subtract the mean from each value and square the result.
  3. Average those squared differences (variance).
  4. Take the square root to get standard deviation.
σ = √[ Σ(x - μ)² / N ]

Example: Test scores 80, 85, 90. Mean = 85. Differences squared: 25, 0, 25. Sum = 50. Variance = 50/3 ≈ 16.67. Standard deviation ≈ 4.08.

Ohm's Law: Understanding Voltage, Current, and Resistance

Ohm's Law is the fundamental equation of electricity: V = I × R, where V is voltage (volts), I is current (amps), and R is resistance (ohms). It's used to design circuits, select resistors, and troubleshoot electronics.

Practical Examples

If a 9V battery is connected to a 3Ω resistor, current I = V/R = 9/3 = 3 amps. If you have a 12V circuit and want 0.5A, you need R = V/I = 12/0.5 = 24Ω.

Kinetic vs Potential Energy: Real-World Examples

Energy exists in many forms, but two of the most important are kinetic (energy of motion) and potential (stored energy). A moving car has kinetic energy; a raised weight has gravitational potential energy.

Formulas

Kinetic Energy (KE) = ½ × mass × velocity²
Gravitational Potential Energy (PE) = mass × gravity × height

Example

A 2 kg ball moving at 3 m/s has KE = 0.5 × 2 × 9 = 9 Joules. The same ball at 2 m height has PE = 2 × 9.8 × 2 = 39.2 Joules.

How to Calculate Your Monthly Loan EMI

EMI (Equated Monthly Installment) is the fixed amount you pay each month to repay a loan. It includes both principal and interest. The formula is EMI = P × r × (1+r)ⁿ / ((1+r)ⁿ - 1), where P = principal, r = monthly interest rate, n = number of months.

Example

Loan ₹5,00,000 at 8% annual (0.6667% monthly) for 5 years (60 months). Monthly rate = 0.08/12 = 0.006667. EMI = 500000 × 0.006667 × (1.006667)⁶⁰ / ((1.006667)⁶⁰ - 1) ≈ ₹10,138.

Understanding Body Fat Percentage: Beyond BMI

Arithmetic Sequences: Finding Any Term and Sum

An arithmetic sequence is a list of numbers where each term increases by a constant difference. Example: 3, 7, 11, 15,... difference = 4.

Important Formulas

n-th term: aₙ = a₁ + (n-1)d
Sum of first n terms: Sₙ = n/2 × (first + last) = n/2 × [2a₁ + (n-1)d]

Example

Find the 10th term of 5, 8, 11,... a₁=5, d=3 → a₁₀ = 5 + 9×3 = 32. Sum of first 10 terms = 10/2 × (5+32) = 5×37 = 185.

Binary to Decimal and Vice Versa: A Beginner's Guide

Computers use binary (base 2) with digits 0 and 1. Humans use decimal (base 10). Converting between them is essential for programming and digital electronics.

Binary to Decimal

Each position represents a power of 2. Example: 1011₂ = 1×2³ + 0×2² + 1×2¹ + 1×2⁰ = 8 + 0 + 2 + 1 = 11.

Decimal to Binary

Repeatedly divide by 2 and read remainders backward. 13 decimal: 13÷2=6 rem1, 6÷2=3 rem0, 3÷2=1 rem1, 1÷2=0 rem1 → 1101₂.
